Sending (Encapsulation)

This is the final step before your data leaves your computer. All the headers and data from previous layers are turned into physical signals:

  • Turns 1s and 0s into electricity, light pulses, or radio waves.
  • Sends data over cables (Ethernet, fiber) or wirelessly (Wi-Fi).
  • Does not care what the data means — it just moves it out.

Think of it like blinking Morse code out of a flashlight — it's just raw transmission.

Receiving (Decapsulation)

This is the first layer your device uses to begin decoding incoming data. It:

  • Receives physical signals through a cable or antenna.
  • Converts them back into 1s and 0s (binary bits).
  • Passes the bits up to the MAC layer (Layer 2) without interpreting them.

Think of this like a light sensor decoding Morse code flashes into actual letters, but not reading the sentence yet.

This marks the transition point — your computer finishes encapsulation by transmitting raw data and starts decapsulation by receiving those same kinds of signals from someone else. It's the bottom and top of the OSI cyclev.
