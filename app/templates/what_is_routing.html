What is a route?

> Let us try to understand the computer's language, in the simplest terms, routes get our data from A to B across the internet. Let us look at the full table below, then back, specifically the IPs: 

D     10.1.3.0/24 [90/30720] via 10.1.1.4, 00:00:05, GigabitEthernet0/2

-> This tells us the following:

  • 10.1.3.0/24 is the destination network (subnet) â€” the range of IPs we want to send data to. Like to Marketing subnet from IT subnet
  • 10.1.1.4 is the next hop from your router to another router (or device like a firewall, switch, or hub) that knows how to get closer to the destination, cycle repeats.
  • Think of it as a breadcrumb for your data to follow. Each router "passes it on" to the next router that knows how to get closer to it's destination.

Ignore the bracketed numbers, the timer, and the interface at the end. Those will be explained in later pages.

Now that we have a basic understanding of what a route is how does the device obtain it? Let us focus on that "D"
